Error Vector Magnitude (EVM) is a critical metric used to assess signal quality in wireless communication systems. It tells us how far the received symbols deviate from their ideal positions in the modulation constellation. A lower EVM indicates better signal integrity and fewer errors during demodulation.

EVM plays a critical role in standards like LTE, 5G NR, and WiFi, which use advanced modulation schemes such as 64-QAM and 256-QAM. These modulation formats offer high data rates by placing more bits per symbol, but they are also more sensitive to noise and distortion. This makes EVM a key parameter in design, calibration, and testing of radios.

Why EVM Degrades

There are two main reasons EVM can degrade. The first is thermal noise, which comes from the radio environment and receiver circuitry. The second is hardware nonidealities such as in-phase quadrature mismatch (IQMM). This post focuses on IQMM which occurs when there is imbalance between the in-phase (I) and quadrature (Q) signal paths, causing an unwanted mirror image of the signal to leak into the system, known as image interference.

For example, an IMRR of 30 dB means that the lowest possible EVM is about -30 dB, regardless of how clean the channel is.

EVM Limits in 5G NR

The 3GPP standard TS 38.101-1 for 5G specifies maximum EVM levels for different modulation formats. These limits must be met by transmitters and receivers to ensure reliable communication. In Frequency Range (FR) 1 (below 6 GHz), the typical EVM targets are:

  • QPSK: EVM must be less than -17 dB
  • 256-QAM: less than -31 dB

In FR 2 (mmWave), implementation is more difficult, so the EVM limits are slightly relaxed.

Takeaway

EVM is one of the most important metrics for evaluating modern communication systems. It reflects both random noise and hardware-induced distortion such as IQ mismatch. Meeting the EVM requirements defined in standards is essential for achieving high throughput, energy efficiency, and system robustness.
